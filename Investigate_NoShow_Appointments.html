Introduction¶

Dataset Description¶

This dataset collects information from 100k medical appointments in Brazil and is focused on the question of whether or not patients show up for their appointment. A number of characteristics about the patient are included in each row. ‘ScheduledDay’ tells us on what day the patient set up their appointment. ‘Neighborhood’ indicates the location of the hospital. ‘Scholarship’ indicates whether or not the patient is enrolled in Brasilian welfare program Bolsa Família(opens in a new tab).

Question(s) for Analysis¶

What factors are important for us to know in order to predict if a patient will show up for their scheduled appointment?

  • Which demographic and health-related factors influence appointment show rates? Consider age, gender, alcoholism, handicap, and health conditions.
  • Does appointment scheduling impact whether patients show up?

Data Wrangling¶

🧹 Data Cleaning Decisions¶

  • Binary Columns Optimization:
    All columns that contain only 0 and 1 (e.g., scholarship, hypertension, diabetes, alcoholism, sms_received, etc.) will be converted to int8 to reduce memory usage. The age column was already stored as int64, which is appropriate

  • Date Formatting:
    The scheduled_day and appointment_day columns were initially stored as strings (object type). They will be converted to proper datetime format using pd.to_datetime() for accurate time-based calculations.

  • Handcap Column Normalization:
    The handcap column contained values greater than 1, although the original dataset defines this column as binary (True/False).
    All values greater than 0 were mapped to 1 to reflect a boolean interpretation of the data.

  • Standardizing Column Names:
    All column names were converted to lowercase and cleaned.
    The column No-show was renamed to no_show for consistency and ease of use in analysis.

  • Duplicate Rows:
    The dataset was checked for duplicates and any were removed to ensure the integrity of the analysis.

In [13]:
binary_cols = ['Scholarship', 'Hipertension', 'Diabetes', 'Alcoholism', 'SMS_received','Handcap']
df[binary_cols] = df[binary_cols].astype("int8") # Data optimization step - changing all binary value - columns to type int8

Handcap Column Inconsistency:
According to the dataset documentation, the Handcap column is intended to be binary, indicating whether the patient is handicapped (0 = no, 1 = yes).
However, we observed rare occurrences of values such as 2, 3, and 4, which are not documented and represent a very small portion of the dataset.
Given their low frequency and the binary nature described in the documentation, it is likely these values were introduced due to data entry errors or mislabeling.
To reflect the original binary intention, we normalized the column by mapping all values greater than 0 to 1.

Conclusion on Data Optimization¶

As part of our data cleaning and optimization process, we have successfully reduced the memory usage of the dataset by adjusting the data types of specific columns. Key improvements include:

  • Binary Columns: We converted columns with binary values (0/1) to the int8 data type, significantly reducing memory usage.
  • Categorical Data: The "No_show" column was converted to a categorical type, further optimizing memory consumption.
  • Datetime Columns: We transformed date columns from the object type to datetime64 to streamline time-based analysis and improve memory efficiency.

Result:¶

  • Before Optimization: 11.8 MB
  • After Optimization: 7.5 MB

These optimizations help improve the efficiency of our analysis and will enable better performance as we work with larger datasets.

Age and Appointment Attendance¶

The average age of patients who showed up for their appointments was approximately 37.8 years, while those who did not show up averaged around 34.3 years. This suggests that younger patients are slightly more likely to miss their appointments than older patients.

Analysis of No-show Rates for Hypertension and Diabetes¶

In this analysis, we explore how the health conditions Hypertension and Diabetes influence appointment no-show rates. The comparison between these two conditions needs to account for their different prevalences in the dataset to ensure a fair analysis.

Key Observations:¶

  • Hypertension is more prevalent in the dataset, meaning that a higher raw no-show rate could be due to the larger number of hypertensive patients.
  • Diabetes, being less common, might have a different no-show rate that is less influenced by the number of patients.

To make the comparison fair, we will normalize the no-show rates for each condition by calculating the proportion of no-shows within each condition group.

Steps:¶

  1. Calculate the no-show rate for patients with Hypertension.
  2. Calculate the no-show rate for patients with Diabetes.
  3. Compare the relative no-show rates between the two conditions.

Let's proceed with the analysis in the next code cell.

Conclusion¶

1. Demographic and Health-Related Factors Affecting Appointment Show Rates:¶

  • Age: Age has a noticeable relationship with the likelihood of showing up for an appointment. We found that patients aged over 34 are more likely not to show up. However, the relationship is not straightforward, and other factors might also be at play.

  • Diabetes & Hypertension: Both diabetes and hypertension were analyzed in relation to appointment attendance. For diabetes, there was a slight difference, with a marginally higher show-up rate for those without diabetes. Hypertension showed no significant effect on attendance rates.

  • Alcoholism: Alcoholism did not have a significant impact on attendance, as the analysis showed a very small difference between patients with and without alcohol issues.

2. Appointment Scheduling Impact:¶

  • Wait Time: Patients who had longer waiting times between scheduling and the actual appointment were less likely to show up. On average, patients who missed their appointments had longer wait times.

Limitations:¶

  • Ambiguity in 'No-show' Label. The No-show column contains 'Yes' for people who didn't show up — which is counterintuitive and can lead to misinterpretation or mistakes in analysis.

  • Limited SMS Reminder Context. We only know whether an SMS was received (SMS_received = 1 or 0).There’s no information about: When the SMS was sent/ If it was actually read/ The message content or frequency. This makes it difficult to evaluate the true effectiveness of the reminder system.

  • Neighborhood as a Categorical Variable. The Neighbourhood column is treated categorically, but: We don’t have geospatial data (e.g., distance to clinic, transportation access).This limits the depth of our analysis about physical accessibility.

  • Imbalanced Classes. The dataset is imbalanced with many more “show” than “no-show” cases. This can bias some statistical interpretations unless properly accounted for.

Key Insights:¶

  • Age and wait time are the most prominent factors influencing whether a patient shows up for their appointment.
  • SMS reminders have a clear positive impact on attendance.
  • Diabetes and hypertension had a relatively small but noticeable influence, while alcoholism did not have much of an effect.
  • External factors, such as neighborhood, highlight a more complex issue that may require further investigation into the underlying causes.
